## Overview

### About the project

The B2C area of the business was marked for strategic growth, which included the optimisation of processes and servicing of clients. "Sport" consists of clients with a national body insurance program with Marsh — meaning the business insures a range of individuals, clubs and organisations related to that sport.

### What we were trying to solve

Internal stakeholders and business partners service these clients and subsequent users on a day-to-day basis. Existing legacy websites existed across multiple platforms with varying levels of functionality, creating a need for brand alignment, content consolidation and platform migration. The scope also needed to allow for onboarding new sport clients in future.

The overall goal of the project was to consolidate public insurance information, the claims process and client servicing requests into one site, creating a scalable user experience.

### Solution — who the site needed to serve

- Individual sports players
- Associations & Clubs
- Marsh brokers (internal users and stakeholders)

### What we needed to answer

1. What do our internal stakeholders need from the site?
2. How do our internal stakeholders engage with the legacy sites?
3. Who is using the website?
4. What do those people need from us?

After this research was complete, we grouped similar users and tasks to create a concise list of actions and information that needed to be contained in the site. This determined how we engaged external support, content writers and internal stakeholders.

## Project Scope & Constraints

- The proposed site had to be built on the existing Adobe Experience Manager platform.
- Preference to leverage existing components within the design system, with the potential for no new components for MVP.
- Any new components or functionality would need to go through a rigorous development cycle.
- Commitment to build the proposed site within six months.

## Research

### What we needed to answer

Initial work for the project included an in-depth evaluation of the five existing legacy sites across nine different sports. The main outcome was to establish a baseline of the existing sites.

### Research actions

1. Extraction of sitemaps
2. Thematic content analysis
3. Functionality scope review
4. Review of Google Analytics data
5. Review of limited data available through existing forms and services

### Findings

1. Information architecture across legacy sites was inconsistent and did not follow a strict hierarchy pattern.
2. Analytics data showed engagement with insurance-themed content was low.
3. Data showed the most engagement was around "Make a claim" and "Certificate of Currency" content and calls to action.
4. The majority of inquiries referred to claims and certificates of currency.
5. Claims were most frequently mentioned by players and club representatives.
6. Certificate of Currency was mentioned exclusively by club officials and representatives.

### Exploring the business model with sport-specific workshops

The goal of the sport-based workshops was to break down ideas into key assumptions and explore the various factors that would determine the potential site. We wanted these workshops to be easy to participate in, since not all internal stakeholders were familiar with this style of work, and to keep focus on the overall picture and goals.

**Goal 1:** High-level overview of the business
**Goal 2:** Evaluate internal and external users

### Questions used to initiate discussion

- Who do you think are the main users of the current site?
- Who do you think should be the users of our current site?
- What do you use the current website for?
- What problem does the current site look to solve for our users?
- How common is this problem across our audience group?
- What differentiates us from our competitors in the market?
- Are there any potential prospects in this audience group?
- How can we identify the specific audience groups we hope to engage with the site?
- What indicators can we use to determine whether the website is working correctly?

### Consolidating and grouping information

After the series of workshops with stakeholders, clear themes and patterns emerged about how they needed to service these clients and how the clients used the site. These were grouped by persona and task to complete, forming the base of the user journeys.

## Define

### Key findings

1. All sports have the ability to service clients in the same way, so the site can reflect that — there is no need for an individual solution per sport.
2. The site needs to serve the broker, club/association and individual. For sports with complex club/player structures, information should be divided per group.
3. Cross-selling product is an important part of the sport business, and the site should be able to facilitate this.

## Reflecting on the Research

### Outcomes & lessons

Validating work externally helped avoid bias from having been involved with the business and its work for an extended period (tunnel vision) — particularly given that there was limited data and analytics available.

With a limited timeline and privacy/data restrictions, surveying or interviewing clubs, club members and individuals (our personas and audience groups) wasn't possible. Being able to reach the primary users we were serving would have been advantageous and given deeper insight.

Given the limited timeline, only brief SEO research was able to be completed. An extensive keyword analysis would have enabled a stronger content and search strategy.
