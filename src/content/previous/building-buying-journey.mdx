## Project Overview

### Building a buying journey within a digital insurance platform

The business had an opportunity to move into direct-to-consumer professional indemnity insurance in the allied health space, with a commitment to producing a market-competitive offering. Given this, the challenge lay in creating an on-par, if not superior, user experience within our technology stack.

## Users & Audiences

To define our audience, we looked at the segments across the insurance product with the most potential by evaluating search volumes and competitors in this space. Our key users were defined as beauticians and physiotherapists.

## Scope & Constraints

What was possible was defined by current technology and the timeframe we were working in. All of the work was subject to sign-off from multiple stakeholders.

## Process

### UX Research

The research began with a full competitor analysis from three different lenses — price, product and UX. We had a unique opportunity to work with an underwriting agency on the product, so this analysis was necessary to create the best product and vessel to sell it.

### Competitor analysis

**Competitors evaluated:** AON, Bizcover, CGU, Allianz

**Aspects considered:**

- Steps and screens for the user
- How long it takes to receive an initial quote and a final quote
- Core products
- Additional products offered and modalities
- Where and how business details are presented
- How personal details are used and followed up
- Additional comments and notes

### Steps & screens — minimising questions & grouping effectively

**Findings**

1. Competitors average 4–5 sections of information per journey.
2. Approximately 4 "submits" / new screens before a quote is displayed — because of mobile focus, users submit more times than they would with a desktop-first approach.
3. Competitors favour clicks and submits rather than scroll.

**Recommendation**

Effectively group and present questions, then use a combination of scroll and submit to create an optimised journey that performs well on both desktop and mobile.

### Core and additional products + modalities

**Findings**

1. All our competitors across allied health (and in other areas) use search as a selection method.
2. We have a wide variety of occupations and selections that the user needs to make and feel confident in, in order to continue and obtain an accurate quick quote.
3. From a legal and insurance-binding perspective, specificity about undertaken activities reduces liability.

**Recommendation**

Develop a search component, similar to those of our competitors, that can facilitate multiple user journeys.

### Collecting personal information

**Findings**

1. Three of the four major competitors required users to submit personal details (name, contact number, address and email) within the first four screens of the journey, before a quote could be generated.
2. AON sent a follow-up email when an application was left incomplete before checkout — indicating that the form captured personal data regardless of whether it was completed.

**Conclusion**

Address and ABN captured upfront can be used for targeting and data collection — worth considering in relation to marketing tactics and personal information. Asking for personal details upfront depends on having the internal support to follow up a lead if the user drops off after viewing the quote. Asking for an email address upfront ensures that if a user drops off when asked for further personal information, there's still a contact to follow up.

**Recommendation**

Ask for sensitive information following the initial quote, before the user is taken to checkout. A/B testing should be conducted to see how the placement of this request performs.

### Learnings from previous work

**Context**

The current online fitness professionals audience is very similar to allied health, so these observations are a good approximation of allied health behaviour.

**Findings**

1. In the last 6 months, 34% of Fitness revenue can be attributed to mobile.
2. Accounting for multi-visit journeys, mobile's contribution to revenue rises to over 40%.
3. Data and behaviour patterns show users come, get a quote, then return to purchase — suggesting the effectiveness of the quick quote in converting users.

**Conclusion**

Desktop and mobile should be treated with equal importance during design and development.

## UI Development

### Final design

The UI development was based on the digital insurance platform, adhering to accessibility standards. The development team worked on creating a user-friendly interface and a seamless buying experience for insurance users.
